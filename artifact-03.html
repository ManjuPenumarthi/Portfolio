AI Opportunity Discovery: Predicting and Preventing Delivery Delays at UPS

ARTIFACT 03

As part of a seven-person AI consulting team (operating as "Vision Flow AI"), I helped apply a structured AI Opportunity Discovery framework to a real logistics problem: delivery delays at UPS. Rather than starting from a machine learning technique, the project deliberately started from the business problem — asking whether AI was actually the right tool before ever proposing one — and worked through business framing, data requirements, risk, and rollout strategy across a written report and an accompanying client-style presentation deck.

Description A team AI-opportunity-discovery project evaluating whether artificial intelligence is a sound investment for reducing delivery delays across UPS's global parcel network. The deliverable included a full written report (Introduction, Business Problem, AI Opportunity, Why AI Is a Good Fit, Value Add, Data Requirements, Potential Challenges, Recommended Next Steps, Conclusion) and a 15-slide client-facing deck built around the same framework under our team identity, Vision Flow AI.
Objective To test a real operational problem — UPS delivery delays — against the specific conditions that make a problem a strong AI candidate (available historical data, genuine multivariable pattern recognition, measurable outcomes, and a demonstrable edge over rule-based systems), rather than assuming AI was the answer by default.
Process
  • Framed the business problem in quantified terms: UPS moves roughly 20.8 million packages a day, so even a 1% miss rate against that volume is 200,000+ late packages daily.
  • Used UPS's own ORION routing system, projected to save $300–$400M per year, as internal proof that this category of AI investment already pays off at UPS's scale.
  • Defined the AI opportunity as a decision-support layer — flagging at-risk deliveries and recommending route adjustments — that keeps dispatchers and drivers as the final decision-makers rather than automating them out.
  • Mapped concrete data requirements (delivery history, live traffic/weather, package data, route/driver data, address quality, failed-delivery records) and named the privacy and security safeguards that data would require.
  • Worked through implementation risk honestly: algorithmic bias against specific neighborhoods or routes, data quality gaps, organizational adoption/trust, and the ethical line against penalizing drivers for conditions outside their control.
  • Proposed a staged rollout — data audit, vendor/data evaluation, stakeholder engagement, then a single-hub pilot — modeled directly on the phased path UPS itself used to roll out ORION.
  • Used the AIML-500 AI Consulting Coach (a custom GPT) for structured research and early drafting support, and Claude AI for slide design and content development on the deck.
Tools & Tech AI Opportunity Discovery framework, business case analysis, AIML-500 AI Consulting Coach (custom GPT), Claude AI, PowerPoint/deck design, APA research & citation
Value Proposition This artifact shows I can operate a step earlier than implementation — scoping whether a business problem genuinely warrants an AI investment, building the case with quantified, defensible evidence, and presenting that case to a non-technical audience. That's the same discipline I'd want applied before proposing any new AI-assisted process inside NetSuite: justify the investment on the problem's own terms first.
Unique Value Rather than treating "AI is a good fit" as a foregone conclusion, we explicitly tested the UPS delay problem against the conditions that make AI worthwhile and grounded every claim in UPS's own public numbers and its own internal case study (ORION), rather than generic industry benchmarks. That evidence-first approach is directly transferable to how I'd pitch any AI-assisted improvement in an ERP environment.
Relevance Before I build anything AI-assisted in NetSuite — whether that's a chatbot, an approval model, or an automation — the real work is the same discovery process shown here: define the business problem precisely, confirm the data exists to support it, and weigh the risk honestly before committing development time.
